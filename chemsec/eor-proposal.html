Section 01
Context & Recommended Structure
Understanding the situation and the right employment approach for Brazil

Jonatan is a Swedish national employed by ChemSec in Sweden. His spouse has accepted an expatriate assignment in Brazil, and he will accompany her for the duration of the posting — approximately two years. Jonatan intends to continue his role at ChemSec from Brazil, which creates a compliance obligation that must be addressed before the move.

Working from Brazilian territory — even fully remotely — means Jonatan generates employment activity in Brazil under a foreign contract. Without a local employment structure, ChemSec operates as an unregistered employer in the country, creating exposure to social security liabilities, corporate tax risk, and potential labor law penalties. This is not a grey area: Brazil's tax and labor authorities treat economic presence seriously, regardless of where the payroll is run.

The recommended structure: Employer of Record (EOR)

Wide Brazil becomes the legal employer in Brazil for the duration of the assignment. Jonatan is issued a CLT employment contract, enrolled in the Brazilian social security and tax systems, and paid locally in BRL. ChemSec retains full operational control — direction, deliverables, schedule — while Wide Brazil carries the legal employment responsibility on the ground.

This is the standard structure for this type of international assignment and the cleanest path to full compliance. It protects ChemSec from unintended legal exposure in Brazil, ensures Jonatan's rights are properly covered under local law, and gives both parties a clear, auditable employment arrangement from day one.

Why not keep the Swedish payroll running?

Brazil establishes tax residency after 183 days of presence in the country. From that point, Jonatan's worldwide income is subject to Brazilian IRRF. Maintaining only a Swedish payroll does not discharge this obligation — it simply leaves it undeclared. The EOR structure ensures Jonatan is correctly registered, taxed, and covered from arrival, with no gap between legal reality and payroll treatment.

How the 13th Salary Works in Brazil

The 13th salary (décimo terceiro salário) is a mandatory annual bonus guaranteed by the Brazilian Constitution. Every employee with a CLT contract is entitled to one full additional month of salary per year, paid in two installments.

First Installment — November
50% of the gross monthly salary, paid by November 30th each year. No income tax on this installment.
Second Installment — December
Remaining 50% of gross salary, paid by December 20th. INSS and IRRF are calculated and deducted on the full 13th amount.
How it is provisioned
Wide Brazil sets aside 1/12 of gross salary each month (plus employer social charges), so the cost is spread evenly across all 12 monthly invoices. No year-end cash spike for ChemSec.
Pro-rated for partial years
If Jonatan's assignment starts or ends mid-year, the 13th salary is calculated proportionally to the months worked in that calendar year.

Wide Brazil contacts Jonatan directly to collect required documentation — passport, Brazilian address, CPF application (if not yet obtained), and bank account details. The CLT employment contract is prepared for his review and signature ahead of the start date.
