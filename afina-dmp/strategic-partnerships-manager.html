Section 01
Client Context & Recommended Structure
Why a phased model fits Afina DMP's entry into Brazil

Afina DMP is entering Brazil to build commercial relationships with the country's leading telecom operators — not to open a local entity at this stage.

Brazil is one of the largest telecom markets in the world, with mobile penetration exceeding one line per person and a small group of dominant operators — Vivo, Claro, and TIM — controlling most enterprise and B2B decision-making. Reaching this market effectively depends less on infrastructure and more on having the right person: someone who already understands how these organizations evaluate new commercial solutions, and who can open doors with senior stakeholders from day one.

Your near-term goal is to hire one experienced Strategic Partnerships Manager to lead pilot conversations with two to three operators, validate commercial traction, and lay the foundation for a broader Brazil strategy. Additional hires and, eventually, a local entity, would only follow once this first phase proves out.

Recommended structure — Recruit, then Employ (BOT model): Wide Brazil first runs a focused search to identify and shortlist candidates with direct commercial experience inside Vivo, Claro, or TIM. Once you select a candidate, Wide Brazil employs them locally through our Employer of Record (EOR) service — so the person is hired compliantly under Brazilian law, without Afina DMP needing to register a local entity. You direct their work 100%; Wide Brazil handles payroll, statutory contributions, and compliance.
Phase 1 · Now
Recruit and hire the first Strategic Partnerships Manager through Wide Brazil's EOR. No entity required. Person starts within weeks, fully compliant under CLT.
Phase 2 · On traction
As pilot projects convert into commercial agreements, scale the team — additional recruitment and EOR hires, same partner, same process.
Phase 3 · On scale
When headcount and revenue justify it, Wide Brazil supports entity formation and transfers employees from EOR to your own Brazilian company — or you can remain on EOR indefinitely.

Why not open an entity now

Entity formation in Brazil takes time and carries fixed obligations regardless of whether the commercial thesis proves out. EOR lets you test the market with full legal compliance and no structural commitment.

Why this person matters more than the structure

Enterprise telecom sales in Brazil runs on relationships built over years inside Vivo, Claro, or TIM. The right hire compresses the time it would otherwise take an outsider to build credibility with these operators.

Why not simply engage this person as an independent contractor (PJ)? Under Brazilian law, a working relationship is legally considered employment — regardless of the contract's label — when it shows personal service (the person can't delegate the work), regularity/continuity, payment for services, and subordination (the company directs how, when, and where the work is done). A Strategic Partnerships Manager working exclusively for Afina DMP, using company tools, following company direction, and reporting into the org structure meets most of these criteria in practice. That combination creates real exposure to reclassification: if a Brazilian court later finds an employment relationship existed, Afina DMP would be liable for the full retroactive package of employee rights (13th salary, vacation, FGTS, social charges) plus potential penalties — this is an active area of Brazilian labor litigation, and a matter still under review by Brazil's Supreme Court (STF, Tema 1389), with no final ruling as of July 2026. Hiring through Wide Brazil's EOR removes this exposure: the person is a CLT employee from day one, with all statutory obligations met and carried by Wide Brazil, while Afina DMP retains full operational control over their day-to-day work.

Section 04
Employee Benefits
Mandatory benefits under Brazilian labor law, plus recommended additions for this role

Brazilian employment law (CLT) requires a defined set of benefits and provisions for every employee, regardless of role or seniority. These are included in the payroll simulation on the following page as pass-through costs — Wide Brazil applies no markup on statutory items.

13th Salary
Mandatory
An additional month's salary paid annually, typically split between November and December. Provisioned monthly as part of employer cost.
Paid Vacation + 1/3 Bonus
Mandatory
30 days of paid annual leave, plus a constitutional bonus of one-third of the monthly salary. Base salary is already covered by monthly invoicing; only the bonus is provisioned separately.
FGTS
Mandatory
A severance guarantee fund equal to 8% of gross salary, deposited monthly into an account in the employee's name.
INSS (Social Security)
Mandatory
Employer contribution covering pension, work-injury insurance, and the "Sistema S" social training system.
Meal / Food Allowance (VR/VA)
Mandatory
A daily allowance for meals, standard across the Brazilian market and expected by candidates at this seniority level.
Life Insurance
Market Standard
Not legally mandatory, but standard practice for professional-level hires in Brazil and included in this simulation.
Health Plan (Plano de Saúde)
Recommended
Not legally required, but one of the most valued benefits in the Brazilian market and close to a baseline expectation for a role at this seniority. Recommended to strengthen the offer for a scarce, senior profile and reduce the risk of losing the preferred candidate to a competing offer. Typical corporate plans for an individual employee run roughly R$ 400–900/month depending on network and coverage level; can be quoted and added to the payroll simulation once a provider tier is chosen.
Variable / Performance Bonus
Recommended
Given the commercial nature of this role, a variable component tied to pilot conversion and contract value is recommended to align incentives with the outcomes Afina DMP needs. This is structured directly between Afina DMP and the employee as part of the offer, sits outside the fixed payroll base used for statutory calculations, and does not change the EOR fee — Wide Brazil can support the disbursement of variable payments through payroll once the structure is defined.
On total cost: The combination of the 36.8% employer social charges, mandatory benefits, and annual provisions typically adds 55–65% on top of gross salary to arrive at total employer cost in Brazil — this is standard across the market and not specific to Wide Brazil's pricing. Health plan and variable bonus, if adopted, would be added on top of the figures shown in the Payroll Simulation.
